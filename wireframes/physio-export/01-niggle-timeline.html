01 · Niggle Timeline

  • The problem it solves: Every physio appointment begins with 15–20 minutes of "When did it start? Which runs? Show me on Strava." Runners struggle to recall specifics — when the pain started, which sessions made it worse, what shoes they wore. The physio is working from incomplete, verbal data.
  • Why this screen matters: This is where the runner sees their body data accumulating into something useful. Each niggle is captured in context — the session it happened on, the shoe, the pace, the severity. Over weeks, a story emerges that no one could reconstruct from memory.
  • The trend alert surfaces patterns automatically. "Left hamstring flagged 4× in 3 weeks, severity increasing" — this is the kind of insight that might take a physio several questions to extract verbally. Here it's visible at a glance.
  • Escalation connectors between same-body-part entries make severity progression visual. The physio can immediately see the trajectory — was it getting worse gradually or did it jump?
  • Shoe data on every niggle entry is deliberate. Many running injuries correlate with shoe mileage or rotation patterns. A physio seeing "Nike Vomero 18 · 342km" next to a hamstring issue gets context they'd never think to ask for.
  • Session type and metrics give the physio biomechanical context. A niggle during 5×800m intervals at 4:02/km tells a completely different story than one during an easy 8km at 5:35/km. Intensity matters for diagnosis.
  • "Share with Physio" CTA uses forest green — a positive, health-associated action — rather than the injury-warning clay/amber. The message: this is a constructive step, not an alarm.
  • For the landing page: This screen shows the depth of data Steady quietly collects through simple post-run check-ins. The value proposition: "You already log this. Now it works for you when you need it most."
